🧺 DIY Camp Laundry

Washing clothes on the road, no laundromat required

Supplies You'll Want

Core Kit

  • 5-gallon bucket with lid (or a bucket washer with plunger insert)
  • Biodegradable camp soap (Campsuds, Sea to Summit Wilderness Wash, or diluted Dr. Bronner's)
  • Mesh laundry bag
  • Paracord + carabiners for a clothesline
  • Clothespins (a few, wind happens)

Nice to Have

  • Clean toilet plunger (drilled with holes) as an agitator
  • Dry bag (doubles as a no-bucket washer)
  • Mesh dish scrub brush for stains/collars
  • Small collapsible basin for rinsing
  • Microfiber towel to press out extra water

Method 1: Bucket Wash

1Fill bucket about halfway with water, add a small squirt of biodegradable soap.

2Add clothes (don't overload, they need room to move), agitate by hand or with the drilled plunger for 3-5 minutes.

3Let soak 15-30 minutes if there's real dirt or odor to work out.

4Dump wash water at least 200 feet from any stream, lake, or water source (see LNT below).

5Refill with fresh water, agitate again for a rinse, dump, repeat once more if suds are still visible.

6Wring by twisting, not squeezing, then hang to dry.

Method 2: Hand Washing (No Bucket)

In a basin or sink

1Fill with water, add a few drops of soap, swish to distribute.

2Submerge the item and scrub fabric against itself at the dirtiest spots (collars, pits, cuffs), or use a small scrub brush.

3For stains, rub a bit of concentrated soap directly onto the spot and let it sit 5 minutes before scrubbing.

4Rinse under running water or in a fresh basin until water runs clear.

5Wring, snap out, hang.

Dry bag / Ziploc method

1Add clothes, water, and a small amount of soap to a dry bag or large ziploc.

2Seal out the air, then knead and shake for 3-5 minutes like a mini washing machine.

3Dump, refill with clean water, shake again to rinse, dump, wring, hang.

This one's great for a single outfit or delicates when you don't want to haul out the full bucket setup.

What Water Is OK to Use

Water Source Use for Laundry?
Potable tap / filtered drinking water Yes, ideal. Safe for wash and rinse.
Campground spigot (labeled potable) Yes, standard for most campground laundry setups.
Collected rainwater Fine for washing, not for drinking unless treated.
Lake, river, or stream water (dipped directly) Don't wash directly in it. Carry water 200+ ft away instead, both for the water source's sake and because soap residue and dirt shouldn't go back in.
Greywater / already-used wash water Don't reuse for rinsing. Fine to dump per LNT guidelines below, not to wash with again.
Non-potable spigots (marked "not for drinking") Skip it if labeled unsafe. Some non-potable sources carry bacteria that can transfer to fabric and skin, especially with cuts or irritation.
Standing/stagnant water Avoid entirely. Mosquito larvae, bacteria, algae.
Soap matters too. Even "biodegradable" soap isn't harmless to aquatic life in concentrated form. It still needs soil and distance to actually break down safely. Always wash and dump well away from any water source, never in it.

Leave No Trace Principles for Camp Laundry

1. Dump water 200+ feet from any water source

That's about 70-80 adult steps. Applies to both wash water and rinse water, even with biodegradable soap.

2. Scatter, don't pool

Broadcast the water over a wide area of durable ground (dirt, gravel, sparse vegetation) rather than dumping it all in one spot. Helps it filter through soil naturally instead of pooling or running off.

3. Skip fragile ground

Don't dump on or near sensitive vegetation, moss, or wetland-adjacent areas. Durable surfaces only.

4. Use minimal, biodegradable soap

A little goes further than you'd think. Concentrated camp soaps are meant to be diluted, more isn't cleaner, it's just more to filter out.

5. Pack out microfiber and lint if possible

Synthetic fabrics shed microplastic fibers when agitated. Not much you can do about it in a bucket wash, but it's worth knowing, and worth choosing natural fibers when you can.

6. Leave your site as you found it

No soap residue on picnic tables or rocks used as scrub surfaces, no clothesline nails in trees, no lingering suds on the ground.
